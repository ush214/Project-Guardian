Booms

Floating barriers used to contain or deflect oil slicks. Proper deployment is critical; effectiveness drops in rough seas or strong currents.

Key Considerations

  • Effectiveness decreases in waves >1 m or currents >1 knot.
  • Requires skilled personnel and specialized vessels.
  • Types: Containment, Sorbent, Fire (heat-resistant).

Best For

  • Protecting shorelines, mangroves, or water intakes.
  • Concentrating oil for recovery.
  • Calm to moderate conditions.

Mechanical Recovery

Removing contained oil from the water surface.

‚ñº

Skimmers

Devices that remove oil from the surface; most efficient on thick, contained slicks.

Key Considerations

  • Types: Oleophilic and Weir.
  • Efficiency depends on oil thickness and viscosity.
  • Water entrainment may require separation.

Best For

  • Large spills in open water with booms in place.
  • Relatively calm sea states.

Sorbents

Materials that absorb oil (pads, booms, loose). Useful for final cleanup or small slicks.

Key Considerations

  • Become hazardous waste when saturated.
  • Labor-intensive to deploy and recover.
  • Natural (peat, straw) and synthetic (polypropylene) options.

Best For

  • Final cleanup of thin sheens.
  • Small, contained spills or hard-to-reach areas.

Changing oil properties using chemical agents.

Dispersants

Break oil into droplets to mix into the water column, accelerating natural breakdown.

Ecological Trade-Offs & Limitations

  • Moves oil into the water column; impacts marine life.
  • Use is restricted in many jurisdictions.
  • Effective only on fresh oil and specific conditions.

Best For

  • Very large spills in deep, open water.
  • When preventing shoreline impact is paramount.

Shoreline Cleanup

Removing oil that has reached the coast.

‚ñº

Manual Removal

Teams using tools like rakes, shovels, and sorbent pads. Highly effective, but resource-intensive.

Key Considerations

  • Large workforce and logistics required.
  • Less ecological damage than aggressive methods.
  • Generates substantial oiled solid waste.

Best For

  • Sensitive areas like marshes and mangroves.
  • Light to moderate oiling.

High-Pressure / Hot-Water Washing

Flushing oil from surfaces (rocks, seawalls) into water for collection by booms and skimmers.

Ecological Trade-Offs & Limitations

  • Highly destructive to surface biota.
  • May drive oil deeper into sediment.
  • Use sparingly and with caution.

Best For

  • Man-made structures (piers, seawalls).
  • Less-sensitive rocky shorelines.
